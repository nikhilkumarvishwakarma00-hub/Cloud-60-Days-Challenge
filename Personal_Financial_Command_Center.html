Investing Basics

Educational overview for when you start earning — not personalized investment advice.

This section is for general financial education only. Claude isn't a licensed financial advisor — the concepts below are factual context to help you evaluate options yourself once you have income to invest.

Core concepts

SIPSystematic Investment Plan — fixed amount invested in a mutual fund at regular intervals, smoothing out market timing risk.
CompoundingReturns earn their own returns over time — the earlier you start, the more time compounding has to work.
Emergency fund3–6 months of expenses kept in a liquid, low-risk instrument before investing in anything volatile.
Risk vs. returnHigher potential return generally carries higher volatility/risk — equity vs. debt vs. fixed deposits.
DiversificationSpreading money across asset types/sectors to reduce the impact of any single investment underperforming.

💡 Financial tips for students

Use the 50/30/20 idea loosely

Even on a small allowance: roughly 50% needs (food, transport, data), 30% wants, 20% savings/goals — adjust the ratio to fit your actual amount.

Track for one full month first

Before tightening any category, log real spending for a full cycle — most budgets fail because they're guesses, not data.

Automate what you can

If a family member transfers your allowance, ask them to split it — one portion straight to savings, so you never "see" it as spendable.

Audit subscriptions monthly

Recurring charges are the easiest budget leak because they don't feel like a spending decision each time they renew.

Small goals build the habit

A ₹1,500 goal you actually hit teaches the savings habit better than a ₹50,000 goal you keep postponing.

Separate "want" from "need" before buying

A 24-hour pause on non-essential purchases above a set threshold cuts a large share of impulse spending.
